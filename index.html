An International African Leader

Adebayo Ogunlesi


–

A Nigerian Lawyer and Investment Banker

    Early Life and Education

    Adebayo had his secondary education at King’s College, Lagos, Nigeria. He then went to Oxford University, England, where he bagged his B.A. with first-class honours in Philosophy, Politics, and Economics. Moving on, Adebayo simultaneously attended Harvard Law School and Harvard Business School, where he graduated with a J.D. magna cum laude and his MBA in 1979.

    Career Life

  • 1980 to 1981 Ogunlesi served as a law clerk to Associate Justice Thurgood Marshall of the United States Supreme Court. Ogunlesi was an attorney in the corporate practice group of the New York City law firm of Cravath, Swaine & Moore, where he had been a summer associate while studying for his M.B.A.
  • 1983 Joined the investment bank First Boston as an advisor on a Nigerian gas project. At First Boston, he worked in the Project Finance Group, advising clients on transactions and financings and has worked on transactions in North and South America, the Caribbean, Europe, the Middle East, Africa and Asia.
  • 1997 to 2002 Head of the Global Energy Group of the by then renamed Credit Suisse First Boston (CSFB).
  • 2002 Appointed as Global Head of CSFB's Investment Banking Division.
  • 2002 Served as a member of Credit Suisse's Executive Board and Management Committee.
  • 2004 to 2006 Executive Vice Chairman and Chief Client Officer of CSFB.
  • 2006 Started a private equity firm, Global Infrastructure Partners (GIP) in July.
  • 2012 Appointed to the Board of Directors of Goldman Sachs in October.
  • 2014 Named Lead Director of Goldman Sachs in July.

When asked about professional success:

“Professional success is about ability, hard work, and relationships. Who you know, and how you connect to them is an incredible determinant of how you would succeed.”

When asked about the role of infrastructure to the economy:

“Poor infrastructure not only inhibits economic growth but quite frankly, it stands in the way and affects the quality of the lives of the people in the continent.”

When speaking on African challenges:

“Each generation of Africans faces a unique set of challenges that defines it.”

Advice to African government on the role of infrastructure to the economy:

“If African governments are serious about encouraging investments in their infrastructure, they have to behave as if they are, and so far, there is little evidence that there is commitment and seriousness.”

When speaking about Entrepreneurship:

“Use your entrepreneurial drive, uncompromising integrity, and a passion for excellence in everything you do, and apply all of those skills, talents and capabilities to address the challenges the continent faces.”

Opinion on change:

“Ordinary people can effect change if they want it badly enough.”
